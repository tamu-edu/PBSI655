Course Description

This course will provide students with a foundational knowledge of the field of cognitive neuroscience. Topics will include methods of cognitive neuroscience, mental simulation, attention, memory, decision making, affect, executive function, motor control, and others. Readings will come from both seminal and recent papers that have made significant contributions to our knowledge of the mind and brain.

Course Prerequisites

Graduate classification

Course Learning Outcomes

After completing this course, a successful student will be able to:

  • list the major pros and cons of the major cognitive neuroscience methods

  • identify major cortical brain areas and networks

  • describe the leading theories of attention, memory, motor control, and executive function

  • describe the neural mechanisms affected by major psychopathology

Textbook and/or Resource Materials

Readings will be posted on Google Drive and will consist of journal articles and book chapters. Weekly reading will consist of 2-3 papers. Most weeks also have a chapter from the Principles of Neural Science. These are supplemental background readings. As preparation, Richard Passinghams’s Cognitive Neuroscience: A Very Short Introduction is highly recommended. For future readings I would also recommend Russ Poldrack’s The New Mind Readers: What Neuroimaging Can and Cannot Reveal about Our Thoughts.

Grading Policy

Grades will be based on discussion posts, quizzes, and presentations.

  • Discussion posts. Prior to each meeting, students will post a discussion response related to the readings (5 points each)

  • In-class quiz. Quizzes will be on the readings to check overall comprehension. Quizzes will be done on canvas at the beginning of each class. These can be done on a phone, tablet or computer (5 point each class)

  • Presentation and discussion leading. On the first day of class we will assign presenters for each article.  Each student will lead a discussion of 2-3 articles over the course of the semester depending on the enrollment. Students can sign up for articles on different dates or multiple articles on the same date.  Students should critically evaluate the material and stimulate discussion. The presenter should only be speaking for 50-60% of the time; participation and discussion by the whole class is expected. See the attached presentation grading rubric. (20 points each)
